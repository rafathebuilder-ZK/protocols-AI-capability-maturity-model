# Moffatt v. Air Canada (February 2024)

## Summary
British Columbia Civil Resolution Tribunal ruling issued February 14, 2024 (2024 BCCRT 149). Air Canada's customer-facing chatbot gave Jake Moffatt incorrect information about the airline's bereavement fare policy; Moffatt relied on the chatbot's statement and later sought the promised refund. Air Canada's defense characterized the chatbot as a "separate entity" whose statements were not attributable to the airline. The tribunal rejected that defense and held Air Canada liable for negligent misrepresentation. Damages awarded: $812.02 (fare difference, pre-judgment interest, tribunal fees).

## Why it matters for the CMM
Canonical Level 2 accountability case with a regulatory overlay. The "separate entity" defense is the precise form of the governance gap this model identifies at Level 2: an AI tool is deployed into a customer-facing position without a protocol assigning liability for its output. The tribunal's rejection is a clean public-record statement that such a gap does not insulate the organization. It is a forcing function for Level 2 → Level 3 transitions in any organization deploying customer-facing AI.

## Claims citable from this observation
- The tribunal held Air Canada liable for negligent misrepresentation based on the chatbot's output
- Air Canada's "separate entity" defense (that the chatbot bore no relationship to the company) was rejected entirely
- Damages awarded: $812.02 (fare difference, pre-judgment interest, tribunal fees)
- Decision date: February 14, 2024
- From Finding Fault Lines (verbatim secondary summary): "The court rejected this argument. It ruled that the chatbot was part of the airline's customer-facing system and that the company was responsible for the commitments it made, regardless of whether those commitments were generated by an AI system."
