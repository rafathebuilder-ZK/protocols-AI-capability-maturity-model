# Armstrong — The Hottest Job in Tech (August 2025)

## Summary
Evan Armstrong's essay frames the Forward Deployed Engineer (FDE) as the role emerging to bridge vendor capability and customer operational reality in the AI era. Draws on OpenAI's own FDE deployment (customers spending $10M+/year), unit economics (FDE compensation $200–300K; services capped at ~25% of Year-1 contract value, implying a ~$1M contract floor), and Workday's 2012 IPO (35% of revenue from on-site professional services) as precedent. Central argument: AI is a platform shift whose binding constraints are operational, not technical — and the FDE is the role that absorbs that operational work.

## Why it matters for the CMM
Anchors the Level 2 → Level 3 transition. Level 2 ("sanctioned but ungoverned") fails when broad tool access collides with undesigned workflow. The FDE function is the empirical shape of the bridge: somebody who embeds with the customer, builds the first governed workflow end-to-end, then hands it to the platform. Armstrong's $1M contract-value floor also explains why this capability has historically been reserved for the largest customers — and why the question of how mid-market orgs cross L2→L3 without an FDE is an open maturity gap.

## Claims citable from this observation
- OpenAI deploys FDEs to customers spending $10M+/year
- FDE compensation is typically $200–300K salary; the services rule of thumb of ≤25% of Year-1 contract value implies an effective $1M+ contract-value floor
- At Workday's 2012 IPO, 35% of revenue came from on-site professional services
- "The largest reason that the FDE has happened today is because AI will end up being a bigger and more important platform shift than the cloud was. The biggest changes are not even technological, they are operational." (verbatim, Armstrong)
