# Report Style Guide — Protocolized Consulting Deliverables
**Applies to:** All client-facing and internal memos, reports, and analytical documents
**Does not apply to:** Templates, workplans, onboarding guides, and project management docs

---

## The Standard in One Sentence

Every sentence must earn its place. If it restates what was just said, hedges a claim that doesn't need hedging, or exists to signal effort rather than convey information, cut it.

---

## Voice and Register

**Use active voice.** "Agencies delay submission because legal review takes six weeks" — not "Submission is delayed due to the legal review process."

**State the finding first, reasoning second.** Readers should be able to stop after the first sentence of any section and know what it says. The rest of the section proves it.

**Write in third person for analysis.** Use "we" only when referring to Protocolized's own analytical choices or commitments ("we hypothesize," "we recommend"). Never "I."

**Be confident, not hedged.** If the evidence supports a conclusion, state it. If the evidence is incomplete, say so once and move on — don't hedge every sentence. "Our research suggests..." once; then write as if the evidence is real.

**Be specific, not gestural.** "Several organizations" → name the count if you know it. "Significant cost" → give the number if you have it. "Some improvement" → say what improved. Vague quantifiers signal absence of evidence; use them only when specificity isn't available.

---

## Structure

### Memos (2–3 pages)
```
Header block        → Prepared by, date, status, version
One-sentence lead   → What this document concludes or recommends
Body sections       → Each opens with the finding; rest of section supports it
What happens next   → Specific decision, action, or question
References          → Only if citations in body
```

### Reports and Analyses (3–5 pages)
```
Header block
Summary             → Findings only, no methodology; 3–5 bullets max
Body sections       → Finding-first structure throughout
Appendix/References → Evidence and citations
```

**Section headers should describe content, not label it.** Not "Analysis" but "Why Sanctioned AI Fails Without Protocol Design." Not "Background" but "The Problem Existing AI Maturity Models Don't Solve." A reader scanning headers should be able to reconstruct the argument.

## AI Writing Patterns to Avoid

These patterns appear frequently in AI-generated text and signal unedited output. Avoid them in all documents, internal and client-facing.

### Negative parallelism

The "not X — it's Y" and "not because X, but because Y" constructions. A single use can sharpen an argument. More than one per document is a mechanical tic, not rhetorical choice.

| Avoid | Replace with |
|-------|-------------|
| "Organizations are not failing because they lack tools. They are failing because..." | "Organizations fail because..." |
| "This is not shadow adoption — it is individuals protecting against..." | "Employees use personal AI tools to protect against..." |
| "Not process compliance, but governance of uncertainty" | "Maturity is defined by governance of uncertainty, not process compliance" |

### Em-dash overuse

Two or three em-dashes per page is a ceiling, not a target. Beyond that, every em-dash should be replaced with a period, comma, colon, or parenthetical — whichever is grammatically accurate. Em-dash pairs (— clause —) inside sentences are especially prone to overuse; the inner clause usually works better as its own sentence.

### "Precisely because" and other assertion adverbs

"Precisely," "fundamentally," "notably," "importantly," and "exactly" claim significance rather than demonstrate it. They are hedge-reversal words: they add rhetorical weight without adding evidence. If the reasoning is sound, the sentence doesn't need the adverb.

| Avoid | Replace with |
|-------|-------------|
| "precisely because probabilistic systems resist deterministic governance" | Explain what the resistance looks like and why it matters |
| "fundamentally different from prior maturity models" | Name what's different |
| "notably, this pattern repeats across sectors" | Name the sectors and the pattern |

### One-point dilution

Making the same argument in four different phrasings across a document. If the thesis appears in the opening, it doesn't need to appear again in every section introduction and again in the conclusion. State it once with precision; cite it briefly when a section adds evidence; don't restate it as if the reader forgot.

### Fractal summaries

Summarizing a section immediately after writing it. If the section is well-structured, the final paragraph is the synthesis — a separate "in summary" block just repeats what was already there. The same applies to recap bullets at section ends and closing paragraphs that restate prior points.

### "Serves as" / "functions as"

Replace with "is." "The model serves as a diagnostic framework" → "The model is a diagnostic framework." The dodge is unnecessary and slows reading.

### -ing phrase appended for shallow analysis

Tacking a present participle clause onto a sentence to inject significance that isn't there: "...highlighting its importance," "...underscoring the need for reform," "...reflecting broader institutional dynamics." If the significance is real, state it as its own sentence with its own argument. If you can't, cut the phrase.

### Bold-first bullets and headers used as substantive structure

Bolding the first phrase of every paragraph or bullet to simulate emphasis. Use bold only for defined terms (first use only) and for the lead finding of a section — not as a formatting habit. When every line starts bold, nothing is emphasized.

### Self-answering rhetorical questions

"What does this mean? It means..." / "The question is whether organizations will act. They will." Nobody asked. State the point directly.

## What to Cut

### Opening throat-clearing
| Cut this | Replace with |
|----------|-------------|
| "This memo seeks to document..." | Start with the content |
| "The purpose of this section is..." | Start with the content |
| "It is important to note that..." | State the point directly |
| "As previously mentioned..." | Don't repeat; trust the reader |
| "In order to fully understand..." | Start with the content |

### Managerial filler
| Cut this | Replace with |
|----------|-------------|
| leverage (verb) | use, apply, draw on |
| stakeholders | name the specific parties |
| challenges | name the specific problem |
| moving forward / going forward | delete; use a date or condition |
| best practices | name the specific practice |
| actionable | say what the action is |
| holistic | say what you mean |
| robust | say what the thing does |
| streamline | say what the process change is |
| ensure | verify, confirm, require — be specific |
| alignment | agreement, shared definition |
| synergies | delete |
| value-added | delete |
| optimize | improve, reduce, increase — be specific |

### Structural bloat
- Don't summarize a section before writing it ("This section covers X, Y, and Z")
- Don't summarize a section after writing it ("In summary...")
- Don't explain what a table shows if the table is clear
- Don't use three adjectives where one will do

## Evidence Standards

**Distinguish hypothesis from finding.** Pre-research: "We hypothesize..." Post-research: "The evidence confirms..." Never present a hypothesis as a finding.

**Cite everything that isn't common knowledge.** Format: Author (Year), short title. Don't use footnotes for substantive points — if it matters, put it in the body.

**Source the numbers.** If you cite adoption rates, productivity figures, or breach statistics, give the source inline. Unsourced numbers will be challenged.

**Attribute case studies to the organization and context, not to individuals.** Use the organization name and the documented incident — not internal sources or inferences.

---

## Formatting Conventions

**Tables** for: parallel comparisons, criteria scoring, level-by-level breakdowns, failure mode summaries. Don't use tables for single-column lists or narrative that needs connecting tissue.

**Bullet lists** for: three or more items that don't require explanation. If each bullet needs a full sentence of context, use short paragraphs instead.

**Bold** for: the first use of a key term being defined, and the lead finding of a section. Not for emphasis generally — if a sentence needs bolding to land, rewrite it.

**Headers** use sentence case, not Title Case For Every Word. Exception: formal document titles.

**No underlining.** No color text. No exclamation points in analytical documents.

---

## Length Discipline

State the target length in the document header and respect it. A 1,500-word blog post is 1,500 words. An 8-page litepaper is 8 pages. Expansion signals one of three problems: unclear thesis, redundant sections, or resistance to making a decision. Identify which one and fix it — don't add pages.

If content genuinely won't fit, restructure first:
1. Cut any section that doesn't advance the central argument
2. Move supporting evidence to an appendix or footnote
3. Only then consider extending the document, and note the extension explicitly

---

## Internal vs. Publication Register

**Internal documents** (model development notes, critique memos, YAML DSL, evaluation criteria) can use sharper analytical language, protocol theory vocabulary, and flag open disagreements directly. The reader is the team.

**Publication-ready documents** (blog post, litepaper, artifact copy) must:
- Avoid internal vocabulary that hasn't been defined for the reader (YAML DSL, bottleneck heterogeneity, stuck pattern guidance)
- Write at the level of a deployment manager or operations VP — not a protocol theorist
- Frame analysis as diagnostic, not prescriptive: the reader places themselves, the model provides the language
- Apply the style guide checks below before any draft goes to review
