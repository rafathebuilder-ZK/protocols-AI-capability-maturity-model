As product innovation plateaus, manufacturers shift to become more dependent on
aftermarket services. Addiction to maintenance revenue creates an opportunity for
disruption.

Every September, the same question resurfaces: is it worth upgrading to the latest
iPhone? Each year’s model offers a slightly better camera, a slightly better battery—
but rarely anything game-changing. Eighteen years after the original iPhone launched,
it’s easy to forget how different things once were. The first iPhone didn’t even have 3G
or copy-and-paste. The iPhone 3G was a breakthrough. The 3GS was radically faster.
The iPhone 4 introduced a front-facing camera. Each release felt like a revolution.
Back then, Apple’s services revenue was tiny—about 6% of the company’s total in 2007.
iCloud was still called MobileMe, and AppleCare+ was an afterthought. Today,
services represent around 22% of Apple’s revenue—about $85 billion annually, almost
four times Apple’s entire 2007 revenue. As the iPhone matured and annual upgrades
became less compelling, Apple pivoted: services, support, and aftermarket
monetization increasingly took center stage.

This shift follows a predictable pattern. Technologies move along an S-curve: rapid
early improvements, followed by a plateau. As breakthroughs get harder, upgrade
cycles lengthen. Through the 1950s, the average commercial airliner stayed in service
for just six years—not because they were worn out, but because each new generation
offered massive leaps in performance and efficiency. Early airplanes, like early
iPhones, had steep S-curves and short replacement cycles.
Here’s the overlooked insight: business models evolve in sync with these S-curves. And
this evolution creates both vulnerabilities and opportunities.
As upgrade cycles slow, companies turn to the installed base for growth. Apple leans
on AppleCare+, iCloud storage, and Apple TV. Jet engine makers earn more from
spare parts and maintenance contracts than from selling new engines—which now are
expected to stay in service for decades. For jets, the aftermarket has become more
lucrative than making and selling the product itself. Apple still makes plenty from
selling new iPhones, but in the jet engine business, things are far worse: engines are
often sold at little or even negative margin, with nearly all profits coming from the
aftermarket. Indeed, makers see themselves discounting engines in order to buy a piece
of the aftermarket.

Your aftermarket profits are my disruptor's opportunity

I believe this aftermarket model is a trap. It locks incumbents into protecting spareparts revenue at the cost of true innovation. A jet engine OEM doesn’t want to build a
dramatically longer-lasting engine—it would cannibalize its spare parts business.
Moreover, the over-pricing of spare parts creates an incentive for third parties to
create knock-off parts. In the case of jet engines, this manifests as allegedly
proprietary alloys protected by trade secret… where sometimes the real trade secret is
that nothing of substance is meaningfully proprietary.
All of this is fine and well so long as the world stays on the flat part of the S-curve.
Customers gradually begin to resent the manufacturers who are milking them on
services while investing little in reliability… but so long as nothing meaningfully better
comes along, vendor lock carries the day.
But: this is where the seeds of future disruption are sown. I believe supersonic is just
such a disruption. We are new just a few years away from a new generation of
supersonic jets entering the market—and putting the world at the start of a new, steep
S-curve in aircraft and propulsion technology. Boom has pledged to run our
aftermarket just above break even, earning our profits through fundamental product
innovation.
The broader principle is simple: when technologies plateau, profit pools migrate to the
aftermarket. That dependency creates a disincentive for incumbents to improve the
core product. New entrants, free from that aftermarket addiction, can attack with
fresh technologies and reset the S-curve. Aftermarket-heavy industries are ripe for
disruption.
Jeff Bezos once said, “Your margin is my opportunity.” The disruptor’s playbook
refines this: your aftermarket profits are my product roadmap.
