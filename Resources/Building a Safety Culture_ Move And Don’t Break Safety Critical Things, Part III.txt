It was Saturday, March 16th, 2024. The XB-1 team and I were deep in the Mojave
Desert, in the final stages of preparing our supersonic demonstrator for its first flight.

First flight is a crucible moment for any airplane. Years of design and analysis all
converge in one instant—when the wheels leave the ground, and reality tests every
decision we’ve made. For XB-1, it was even more intense: this was the first airplane
built by a brand-new company. That first takeoff would instantly tell us if we’d
overlooked something critical.

That morning at our daily stand-up, I reminded everyone that while we were close to
the finish line, safety came first.
“If you find a problem with the airplane,” I said, “I need to know about it. I might say a
four-letter word—but then I’ll say thank you. And I’ll ask: what do you need to fix it?”
That day XB-1 completed its final high-speed taxi test. The weather for the next
morning looked perfect. The entire team could sense what was coming.
Then, that afternoon, one of our young engineers—Grace—walked into my office.
“Blake,” she said, “we’ve got a problem. The drag chute isn’t working consistently. I
think we need to fix it before we fly.”
I might indeed have muttered a four letter word—but I also thanked her. Over the next
several days, the whole team rallied behind Grace. We tore down the drag-chute
system, found the root cause, fixed it, and reassembled. XB-1 flew safely eight days
later, on March 22nd, 2024.
The drag chute wasn’t strictly necessary—it’s an emergency brake. It turned out we
never actually needed it anywhere in the flight test campaign. But it was there for a
reason—the chance that we might need it. That Saturday, had I shrugged and said, “fly
anyway,” it would have been catastrophic in a different way. We’d have set the
precedent that when pressure mounts, the safety bar can move. And that destroys a
safety culture faster than any mishap ever could.
Grace did the hardest thing you can do in an aviation startup: she brought bad news at
the worst possible time. And with her courage, she made us all safer and stronger.
We celebrated her in front of the company. Because at its core, safety culture is defined
by how leaders respond when someone brings bad news. Most people instinctively avoid
disappointing leadership. So when someone has the courage to raise a red flag, the
only right answer is gratitude—and immediate action.

Safety Culture: Easy to Break, Hard to Build
I’m immensely proud of the safety culture we’ve built at Boom.
A generation ago, Boeing led the world in aviation safety. Today, although air travel
remains the safest mode of transport by far, we’ve seen tragic and entirely preventable
lapses that remind us safety culture is broken more easily than built.
I’ve come to believe that safety culture comes down to two key elements: one
behavioral, one philosophical.
1. How leadership responds when employees surface problems.
Every employee must know they’ll be thanked—not punished—for raising an
issue.
2. How we accept risk.
“Safety” doesn’t mean the absence of risk or attempting to eliminate all
conceivable risks. It means being open and deliberate about the risk we accept.

Early on, I wrestled with this question. Declaring that safety is the top priority sounds
good—but if interpreted literally, I worried it would be paralyzing. If the safest option
is always “don’t fly,” we’d never build anything new.
Innovation, especially in aerospace, requires taking risks. But the goal isn’t to
eliminate all risk—it’s to decide what kind and level of risk is acceptable—and then
take only those risks.
That led me to a more nuanced view: Safety is best understood as taking contextuallyappropriate risk. Putting safety first means taking risk seriously—identifying risks
consciously, quantifying them whenever possible, and implementing whatever
mitigations are necessary to meet a defined safety bar.
Consider this: In the U.S., auto accidents kill the equivalent of two fully loaded 737s
every 48 hours. Yet we accept driving as “safe” because it’s necessary, and for now
there’s no lower-risk alternative for daily life. By contrast, the original 737 MAX,
despite being statistically much safer than driving, was appropriately judged unsafe—
because its design flaws were avoidable, and safer alternatives existed.
Safety isn’t an out-of-context absolute: It involves benefit relative to risk, judged in the
context of practical alternatives. Something is “safe” when its benefits justify its risks,
and no less-risky alternative is practically available. This framing changes everything
—and actually increases safety.

Defining a Risk Posture
At Boom, we formalize this idea. Safety means defining a risk posture—then
consciously and transparently taking only those risks consistent with it. We define a
risk posture for every major effort—and then consciously assess and prioritize risks
relative to our standard.
For example:
XB-1: Our experimental jet, flown by a world-class test pilot, was designed with a

maximum acceptable catastrophic-failure risk of 1 in 100,000 flight hours (10 ).
That’s appropriate for an R&D program exploring new technology.

no worse than 1 in 1,000,000,000 (10 )—the same as any other airliner. While speed
is immensely important, we don’t think a doubling speed justifies increased
passenger risk—and so we aim to meet or exceed the safety bar achieved by every
other airliner.
This is the key: Safety doesn’t mean no risk—it means no unexamined risk. It’s about
being intentional and data-driven in what we accept, and transparent about why.

The Pillars of Safety at Boom
After years of iteration, we’ve built several core practices into our DNA:
1. Safety means conscious risk-acceptance, judged relative to a contextuallyappropriate risk posture.

We talk openly about risk and ensure it’s consistent with our posture. “Safety
first” means “be intentional,” not “never take risk.” Our test pilots take a very
different level of risk than our passengers will.
2. Radical transparency.
We encourage everyone to surface safety concerns. We also maintain an
anonymous reporting system as a backup—used rarely now, because trust runs
deep.
3. Continuous learning and open sharing.
We discuss safety at every all-hands, share lessons learned, and treat near-misses
as seriously as incidents.
4. Celebrating the right behaviors.
We publicly recognize employees who raise issues, like Grace—because surfacing
problems is a sign of strength, not weakness.
5. Go/no-go decisions are made independently by the team involved.
I was never in the room for an XB-1 go/no-go decision. We trust the team to make
the right decision without senior leadership putting their thumb on the scale.
